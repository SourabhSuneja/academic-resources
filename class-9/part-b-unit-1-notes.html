Introduction to Data - Notes

1. What is Data?

Data refers to raw facts and figures that have no meaning on their own.

Example: Numbers like 97, 85, 76, or words like "John", "Blue", "2pm".

2. What is Information?

Information is processed, organised and meaningful data used for understanding and decision-making.

Example: John's scores in Math are 97, 85, and 76. This tells us something useful.

3. Indian Information Technology Act, 2000

This act gives legal recognition to electronic records and digital signatures and helps prevent cybercrimes.

  • Protects sensitive personal data.
  • Recognises electronic contracts.
  • Punishes cybercrime and data theft.

According to the Act: Data or information includes recorded facts, figures, messages, documents, and emails.

4. Information Concept and Processing

Information is created when data is collected, processed, stored, and shared using technology like computers and networks.

5. Qualities of Good Information

  • Relevance: It should match the need.
  • Availability: Easy to access when required.
  • Timeliness: Available at the right time.
  • Accuracy: Correct and error-free.
  • Completeness: Should cover everything needed.
  • Meaningful & Action-Oriented: Should help in decision-making.
  • Conciseness: Brief but complete.
  • Commensurate: Should match with the problem or need.
  • Confidence in Source: Trusted source of data.

6. What is Information Technology (IT)?

Information Technology is the use of computers, networks, and software to process and manage information.

7. Objectives of IT

  • To make data handling faster and easier.
  • To help in decision-making using accurate information.
  • To improve communication and productivity.

8. Data-Information-Knowledge-Wisdom (DIKW) Model

The DIKW model shows how data becomes wisdom through steps:

  • Data: Raw facts and values (Example: 37°C, 38°C).
  • Information: Processed data with meaning (Example: Body temperature readings).
  • Knowledge: Understanding what it means (Example: These temperatures show fever).
  • Wisdom: Taking correct action using knowledge (Example: Go to the doctor and take rest).

Real-Life Example:

A weather app collects temperatures (data), shows tomorrow will be 44°C (information), you know it's a heatwave (knowledge), so you plan to stay indoors and drink water (wisdom).

9. Applications of DIKW Model

  • Used in business to make smart decisions.
  • In schools for teaching using insights from student data.
  • In hospitals for better diagnosis and treatment planning.

10. Limitations of DIKW Model

  • Sometimes too simple to show real-life complexity.
  • Wisdom can vary from person to person.
  • Depends on good data and correct processing.

11. Knowledge-Based Industries & Peter Drucker

These are industries where knowledge and skills are more important than machines.

Peter Drucker called such workers as Knowledge Workers – people who use brains more than hands.

  • Examples: IT companies, research labs, education, healthcare, finance.

12. Applications of Data in Different Fields

  • Healthcare: For patient records and diagnosis.
  • Online Shopping: To show personalized recommendations.
  • Education: To track student progress.
  • Travel: For booking and route suggestions.
  • Entertainment: For song/movie suggestions.

13. What are Digital Footprints?

Digital Footprints are the traces of your online activity like websites visited, posts shared, searches done etc.

14. Active vs Passive Digital Footprints

  • Active: Data you knowingly share online.
    Examples: Posting a photo, commenting, signing up on websites.
  • Passive: Data collected without you knowing.
    Examples: Websites tracking your clicks, location collected by apps.

15. Data Loss and Recovery

Data loss can happen due to virus, system crash, accidental delete or hardware failure.

Data recovery means getting back lost data using tools or backups.

  • Always back up important files.
  • Use antivirus and cloud storage.
  • Recovery tools help restore deleted files.
