Arranging and Collecting Data - Notes

1. What is Data Collection?

Data collection is the process of gathering information from different sources to study, analyze and make decisions.

2. Types of Data Collection Methods

  • Interviews: Face-to-face or online conversations to collect opinions and facts.
    Advantage: Detailed responses.
    Disadvantage: Time-consuming, prone to bias.
  • Questionnaires: Written set of questions given to people.
    Advantage: Can reach many people. Disadvantage: May get incomplete responses.
  • Surveys: Collecting data from a group of people about their choices or experiences.
    Advantage: Quick and covers large audience. Disadvantage: Limited depth.
  • Case Studies: Detailed study of a person, event or group especially for research and hypothesis testing.
    Advantage: In-depth understanding. Disadvantage: Cannot be generalized for everyone.
  • Focus Group Discussions: Small group talks guided by a facilitator.
    Advantage: Multiple opinions at once. Disadvantage: May be influenced by dominant voices.
  • Smart Gadgets & Devices: Data collected from watches, phones, or IoT devices.
    Advantage: Real-time data.
    Disadvantage: Privacy issues.

3. Primary Data vs Secondary Data

Primary Data: Data collected directly by the researcher.
Example: Surveying students about their study habits.

Secondary Data: Data already collected by others.
Example: Using government reports or online articles.

4. Types of Data

  • Constant: Values that never change.
    Example: Number of days in a week = 7.
  • Variable: Values that can change.
    Example: Marks of students in a test.

5. Variable Types

  • Numerical: Expressed in numbers.
    • Discrete: Whole numbers only (Example: Number of books).
    • Continuous: Includes fractions/decimals (Example: Height, weight).
  • Categorical: Based on labels or categories (Example: Gender, Blood group). Gender can only have these pre-set "categories": Male or Female.

6. Quantitative vs Qualitative Data

Quantitative: Data in numbers (Example: Age = 15 years).
Qualitative: Data in words or categories, describing a quality. Can't be measured in numbers. (Example: Eye color = Blue).

7. What is Big Data?

Big Data refers to large and complex sets of data that are difficult to process using traditional methods.

8. 3V’s of Big Data

  • Volume: Huge amount of data.
  • Velocity: Speed at which data is generated.
  • Variety: Different forms of data (text, images, videos).

9. 5V’s of Big Data

  • Volume
  • Velocity
  • Variety
  • Veracity: Trustworthiness of data.
  • Value: Usefulness of data.

10. Big Data Types

  • Structured: Organized data like given in tables with a proper structure.
    Examples: Bank transactions, students records in a school software.
  • Semi-Structured: Partly organized.
    Examples: Emails, XML files.
  • Unstructured: No fixed or pre-defined format.
    Examples: Videos, social media posts — scattered on different pages, in different languages or in different formats.

11. Applications of Big Data

  • Retail: Personalized shopping suggestions, smartly setting discounts for different customers.
  • Science: Studying space, genetics, or climate change.
  • Sports: Tracking player performance, F1 car sensors.
  • Healthcare: COVID-19 tracking, patient monitoring, drug discovery, diagnosis.
  • Social Media: Showing personalized content, advertisements, pages etc.

12. Classification

Classification means grouping data into categories.
Example: Sorting emails as “Spam” or “Not Spam”.

13. Regression

Regression finds the relationship between variables.
Example: Predicting marks based on hours of study.

14. Clustering

Clustering means grouping similar data points together without predefined labels.
Example: Grouping customers based on shopping behavior — those who purchased electronics in one group while those who purchased cosmetics in another group.

15. Reinforcement Learning

A type of machine learning where systems learn by trial and error using rewards and punishments.
Example: A robot learning to walk — every time it takes a wrong step, the software "punishes" it so that it improves the next time.

16. Univariate vs Multivariate Data

Univariate Data: Involves one variable.
Example: Heights of students.

Multivariate Data: Involves two or more variables.
Example: Heights and weights of students together.
