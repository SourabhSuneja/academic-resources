Data Visualizations - Notes

1. What is Data Visualization?

Data Visualization is the representation of data using charts, graphs, and maps.

It helps the human brain understand complex numbers easily by turning them into visual pictures.

2. Importance and Applications

Visualizing data helps us spot trends and patterns that we cannot see in simple text or tables.

  • Sales Growth Patterns: Companies use line graphs to see if sales are going up or down over months. It helps them decide which product is popular.
  • Monitoring Student Growth: Teachers can use bar charts to compare a student's marks in different terms to see if they are improving.
  • Impact of Pandemics: During COVID-19, graphs helped people understand how fast the virus was spreading.

  • Perform calculations using formulas.
  • Generate graphs and charts automatically.

4. Different Types of Charts

Different data needs different types of charts to be understood clearly.

  • Dot Plot: Uses dots to show the frequency of data. Good for very small amounts of data.
  • Bar/Column Chart: Uses rectangular bars. Best for comparing different categories (e.g., likes for different fruits).
  • Pie Chart: A circle divided into slices. It shows percentages or parts of a whole (e.g., Market Share).
  • Histogram: Similar to a column chart but used for continuous data ranges (like age groups 10-20, 20-30). The bars touch each other because there are no gaps in the ranges.

5. Shapes of Histograms

When we plot data on a histogram, the overall shape tells us a story about how the data is distributed.

A. Normal Distribution (Bell Curve)

Most of the data is clustered in the middle, and it decreases symmetrically on both sides. It looks like a bell.

Example: Height of adults (most are average, few are very short, few are very tall).

B. Bimodal Distribution

This graph has two distinct peaks. It suggests there are two different groups mixed in the data.

Example: Test scores where one group studied hard (high peak) and another didn't study at all (low peak).

C. Right-Skewed (Positive Skew)

The "tail" of the graph stretches out to the right side. Most data is bunched up on the left.

Example: Income distribution (Most people earn typical wages, a few very rich people pull the tail to the right).

D. Left-Skewed (Negative Skew)

The "tail" of the graph stretches out to the left side. Most data is bunched up on the right.

Example: Age at retirement (Most people retire when older, very few retire young, pulling the tail left).

E. Random (Uniform) Distribution

There is no clear pattern or peak. All bars are roughly the same height.

Example: Rolling a fair die 600 times (each number 1-6 should appear roughly 100 times).

6. Single Variable vs Multi-Variable Plot

Single Variable (Univariate) Multi-Variable (Multivariate)
Analyses only one type of data. Analyses two or more types of data together.
Example: A chart showing just the temperatures recorded over a week. Example: A chart comparing Temperature AND Humidity over the same week to see if they are related.

7. Collecting and Analysing Data

  • Collecting Data: Gaining facts and figures from sources like surveys, sensors, interviews, or observations.
  • Analysing Data: The process of cleaning and inspecting the collected data to discover useful information and make decisions.

8. Types of Data

Data can be classified based on its nature.

Numerical vs Categorical

  • Numerical Data: Data expressed in numbers. You can perform calculations on it.
    Example: Height (170cm), Weight (65kg), Price ($50).
  • Categorical Data: Data that represents groups or labels. It is descriptive.
    Example: Color (Red, Blue), Gender (Male, Female), Pass/Fail.

Homogenous vs Heterogeneous

  • Homogenous Data: A set where all data items are of the same kind/type.
    Example: A list containing only integers: [5, 12, 33, 45].
  • Heterogeneous Data: A set where data items are of mixed types.
    Example: A single student record containing Name (text), Age (number), and Attendance percentage (decimal).
